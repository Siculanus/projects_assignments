STEP 3:


Concerning a simple table such as the one used here, both OpenOffice and Tableau are fairly easy to use. It is interesting that the column chart was not available for use with this table on Tableau, which led me to explore different charts. Both Line and Columns are perfect for this type of data.

Flexibility with OpenOffice is also limited. Basic charts can be built easily without much fuss. Changes can be done to texts, headlines, size and color, even position. There is no doubt however that Tableau Public offers a variety much bigger of options, including different types of charts. Files can also be saved in both in a variety of formats allowing to be used according to needs.

Regarding graph ability, OpenOffice provides us with basic variations, such as including 3D format, changing the shape of lines, selecting the piece of data to be used for the graph and the position. Tableau, as it can already be seen from the images, provides the graphs with more clear legends. It can also be more artistic, while OpenOffice is way more functional.



STEP 4:



In terms of ease of use, both can be very easy to use. It can also depend on what people search for. Spreadsheets help build data tables and from there get additional help with some visualization. Tableau is especially made for the visualization and it depends on data already built and imported. There is no doubt that Tableau is more open for exploring the artistic side of the graph and explore a variety of shapes, forms and colors.
